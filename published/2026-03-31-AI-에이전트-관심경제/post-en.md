# Where Did All the Ads Go?

## I Handed Over the Search Bar

These days, I barely search anymore. To be precise, I've stopped doing the old-school thing — typing words into a search bar, cracking open twelve tabs, then clicking some link buried in the third row of results. Now I just ask Claude or Gemini. They go off and dig around, make sense of things, and come back with a tidy summary. I sit there and receive the answer. It's pretty convenient. Shockingly convenient, honestly. Once you've tasted this level of ease, you lose all confidence in your ability to ever scroll to page two of search results again.

So I drew a simple conclusion: if people search less, search ads should fall. This felt less like a theory and more like a household rule. Open the fridge less, lower your electricity bill. Take fewer buses, spend less on transit. Search less, run fewer search ads.

The world, however, occasionally makes our common sense look a little embarrassing.

## Down, But Also Up

In February 2024, Gartner forecast that traditional search engine volume would drop 25% by 2026. People were already migrating to answer engines, the logic went. So far, my prediction seemed to be holding up. Then Alphabet dropped its Q4 2025 earnings in February 2026, and the whole vibe shifted. Search advertising revenue: $63.1 billion. Up 17% year-over-year. An all-time high.

When I saw that number, I paused. And for a brief but sincere moment, I wondered whether I had fundamentally misunderstood what advertising even is. This kind of thing is not entirely new to me. I am also a person who still harbors mild suspicion toward the defrost button on the microwave.

The secret turned out to be simpler than I expected. Google layered AI Overviews on top of its search results. If people are reading AI summaries instead of clicking blue links — well, just put ads inside the summaries. According to Semrush, the share of AI Overviews featuring ads jumped from 5% in March 2025 to 26% by October. That's a 394% increase in eight months. People clicked fewer links, but ads didn't get sold any less. If anything, they got more concentrated inside the platform.

## Advertising Loves Wherever Attention Goes

When you think about it, none of this is surprising. Advertising was never in love with search. It's in love with the places where attention gathers and decisions get made. In the 1950s, that was TV. In the 2000s, it was the search bar. In the 2010s, it was the social feed. Every time a new medium appeared, the surface area for advertising grew — and so did the number of advertisers. Once, only big corporations could buy ads; a 15-second TV spot cost a small fortune. Today, a neighborhood hair salon, an Etsy seller, a solo brand built by one person — anyone can run an ad for ten bucks.

In December 2025, dentsu projected that global ad spending would exceed $1 trillion for the first time in 2026. Numbers are usually cold and clinical, but sometimes they read like poetry. "One trillion dollars" feels like the accounting version of the sentence: we have an extraordinary talent for selling each other's attention.

Asking why ad spending hasn't shrunk even as search has is a little like asking why the ticket counter at the train station is more crowded now that fewer people are walking through the main hall. The traffic patterns changed. AI conversation, meanwhile, is creating more foot traffic than any medium before it. Infinite conversation, infinite questions, infinite answers. Every moment attention lands somewhere, a billboard goes up.

## An Offer That's Hard to Refuse

So the decision to put ads inside AI answers is, from a business standpoint, genuinely hard to turn down.

In February 2026, a few hours before the Super Bowl, Anthropic aired an expensive ad. A man on screen asks an AI for help communicating with his mother. The AI begins with warm advice — then pivots, smoothly and without warning, to recommending a dating site. Text on screen: "Ads are coming to AI. But not to Claude." There's a quiet irony in there: using an ad to advertise that you don't run ads.

That same night, ChatGPT got ads. Sam Altman posted on X that Anthropic's ad was "funny but clearly dishonest." Six weeks later, ChatGPT's ad pilot was tracking above $100 million in annualized revenue. Internal documents reportedly show OpenAI projecting ad revenue of $25 billion by 2029. Staring at a number like that and saying "nope, ads still aren't for us" is not an easy sentence to get through.

Running frontier AI models costs an astronomical amount of money. Companies that can credibly say "we don't do ads" are the ones that don't need ad money to survive. Whether Anthropic's stance is a principled bet or a privilege made possible by its funding structure — it's too early to say. But the size of the temptation is not in question.

## The Target Is Changing

Up to here, the story is a familiar one. New medium arrives, ads follow. Radio, TV, the internet, AI. No twists.

But the genuinely interesting development is something else. Imagine a world where an agent does your shopping. You say "get me a humidifier under a hundred bucks" and the agent compares options, picks one, and places the order. You never click a link. You never visit a comparison site. So — who is a brand's advertising actually aimed at? If you build an ad for me, I won't see it. The agent sees it first, the agent judges it, the agent decides.

If ads aimed at humans speak the language of taste and feeling, ads aimed at agents speak the language of parameters. Not "this is effortlessly stylish" but "100% cotton, 2-hour delivery, 5% below market low, return rate 0.3%." Agents are not moved. Agents process inputs.

This is already the battlefield the industry is gearing up for under the name GEO — Generative Engine Optimization. Think of it as SEO's successor. It's about becoming the default source an agent consults, buying priority in the agent's decision tree. It's the same logic as Google paying Apple billions every year to be the default search engine on iPhones. That's the new advertising. Not B2C. B2A — Business to Agent.

A human can look at an ad, think "that seems overpriced," and still click out of curiosity. But if an agent classifies a product as "poor value," that product simply never reaches my screen. There's no impression to make. You can persuade a person, but once you fall outside an agent's ranking criteria, getting back in is a different kind of problem.

## The Subject of Attention Is Changing

What makes this shift strange is that the web's first reader is changing too. Imperva's 2025 report found that 51% of internet traffic now comes from bots — the first time in internet history that bots have outnumbered people. Entities that don't eat, don't use the bathroom, and don't slip away during the Super Bowl halftime show now account for the majority of the web.

Attention has long been a strictly human resource. You have 24 hours. You choose what to look at and what to scroll past. That's attention. Advertising was the art of capturing it, stealing it, shaking it awake. But I've already started handing parts of my attention over to agents — what to read first, what to summarize, what to skip, what to recommend.

Capital has always followed whoever holds the decision-making power. Once, it followed my eyeballs. Now it follows the answer window. Soon, it will follow whatever sees things on my behalf before I do.

And someday, your shopping agent will probably say something like: "I found three options that match your criteria. The first has the best price, the second has the fastest shipping, and the third — I'm not entirely sure why it's here, but it's sponsored."
