# Why Do Fish Look the Same on Both Sides?

There are moments when diving that your mind starts to wander in strange directions. At around ten meters below the surface, a fish glided past me, and I found myself thinking: why does it look like that? What's the reason both sides are mirror images of each other? I can't say whether anyone else has ever entertained this kind of question at ten meters underwater, but once it surfaced in my head, it refused to go away.

Water is a three-dimensional space. You can go up, down, forward, backward, left, right — any direction you like. And yet nearly every creature living in it happens to be bilaterally symmetrical. There are radially symmetrical animals, and irregular ones too, so why have the most successful animals in the ocean ended up looking like they were folded down the middle? This can't be coincidence. Nature doesn't repeat itself this consistently by accident.

## Gravity and Light Split the World in Two

There's something easy to forget when you're underwater. It may look like a true three-dimensional playground, but two very powerful axes dominate the aquatic world. The first is gravity, and the second is light. Gravity pulls downward; light comes from above. Together, these two forces make "up" and "down" into completely different environments. The upper water column is bright and exposed — easy pickings for predators. The lower depths are dark, and at the bottom there's a solid floor. Almost every fish, then, has a back that looks nothing like its belly. The back is dark; the belly is pale. Seen from above, the fish blends into the dark water below. Seen from below, it disappears against the bright surface above. This is dorsoventral asymmetry, and it is not an accident.

When I first read this explanation, I felt the satisfying click of a puzzle piece locking into place. I immediately turned to my wife and asked whether she knew why fish have dark backs and light bellies. She studied me for a moment, then mentioned that we were out of milk.

Anyway. Carrying on. That leaves left and right. And front and back are already accounted for by the direction of travel — any creature that moves forward is going to end up with a head at one end and a tail at the other. So the only axis that remains naturally symmetrical is left to right. Now, what happens if an animal is asymmetrical along that axis? It experiences a constant rotational force to one side. A creature that wobbles and veers while swimming cannot escape predators. Bilateral symmetry isn't a design choice — it's a constraint imposed by physics.

When I worked this out, I felt a small, private glow of satisfaction. A throwaway question from ten meters down had resolved into something this clean. Of course, I hadn't discovered anything. Nature had sorted all this out several hundred million years ago. I was simply a very late arrival to the party.

## When Life Came Ashore, the Brain Got Some Breathing Room

But the story doesn't end there. The more interesting part is still to come.

About 370 million years ago, some fish hauled themselves out of the water and onto land. This is one of the most dramatic events in the history of vertebrate evolution. But look at it from the perspective of movement, and something peculiar becomes apparent. These animals moved from a three-dimensional environment to a two-dimensional one. In water, they could swim in any direction — up, down, forward, backward, sideways. On land, they were essentially stuck on a flat surface. They could jump, but they couldn't stay airborne for long. Gravity kept them firmly on the ground.

Why does this matter? Consider it from the brain's perspective. Coordinating three-dimensional movement in water requires staggeringly complex real-time calculations — speed, direction, buoyancy, current, rotational forces, all processed simultaneously. On land, this workload drops dramatically. You don't need to think much about vertical movement. All that neural bandwidth that had been consumed by three-dimensional navigation was suddenly free.

Free for what, exactly? For predicting the future, planning ahead, and cooperating with others. The conditions were in place for intelligence to accelerate rapidly. Coming ashore wasn't simply a change of address. It was a liberation of the brain.

## The Octopus: A Genius Who Took a Different Road Entirely

At this point, one creature absolutely demands to be introduced. The octopus. Discussing the evolution of intelligence without talking about the octopus is a bit like teaching English literature and somehow leaving out Shakespeare.

The octopus brain contains roughly 500 million neurons. That's a respectable number. For comparison, dogs have around 530 million, cats around 250 million. The octopus, which isn't a vertebrate by any stretch of the imagination, is operating at roughly the same neural scale as a dog. But the truly astonishing thing isn't the total count — it's where those neurons live. Only one-third reside in the central brain. The remaining two-thirds are distributed across the eight arms.

When I first learned this, I sat with it for a moment. The arms think. If you sever one of an octopus's arms, it continues to move on its own for some time afterward, making autonomous decisions without any input from the central brain. I should note that I have two legs in full working order and I still manage to miss steps on staircases. The octopus has eight arms that each think independently and somehow never get tangled up with one another. This is not a flattering comparison for me.

The story of Inky has become something of a legend in conversations about octopus intelligence. In 2016, a common octopus at the National Aquarium of New Zealand squeezed through a small gap at the top of his tank, made his way across the floor, found a drainpipe that led to the sea, and escaped. The keepers arrived the next morning to an empty tank and a faint damp trail on the floor. This implies that Inky had scouted his escape route in advance. I regularly forget what time hotel checkout is. Inky identified a drainpipe, waited for the right moment, and executed a nighttime breakout.

Inky wasn't a one-off. Researchers at the Seattle Aquarium were once baffled by a fish-disappearing mystery. Each night, the population in one of the tanks was diminishing, but no one could work out why. They eventually installed a night-vision camera and caught the culprit: a Giant Pacific octopus who had been lifting the lid of its tank each night, climbing out, catching fish from a neighboring tank, returning to its own tank, and closing the lid behind it. A perfect crime, with forensic evidence carefully destroyed.

Why did the octopus become so intelligent? It has no skeleton. No skeleton means that, in theory, any part of the body can bend in any direction. The degrees of freedom approach infinity. This makes for extraordinary physical versatility, but it also means the body is extraordinarily difficult to control. The computational difference between directing a robot arm with a fixed number of joints and directing an arm made of soft, boneless, infinitely pliable flesh is not a small one. The octopus solved this problem with distributed intelligence. It resembles the way a company might function when a CEO sets overall strategy and lets each team handle its own execution — or, more precisely, it resembles the architecture of the internet, where no single node controls everything. The octopus's central brain decides *what* to do; the individual arms figure out *how* to do it. This arrangement frees up the central brain for higher-order tasks: opening jars, using tools, escaping from aquariums.

## So Why Didn't They Build Civilizations?

The octopus is startlingly clever. It can learn mazes, open screw-top bottles, devise hunting strategies, and change the color and texture of its skin to match different environments in an instant. Researchers have even found evidence that octopuses dream. While sleeping, their skin pulses through rapid changes in color and pattern — as if something is being experienced. This is either fascinating or slightly unsettling, depending on your disposition.

And yet octopuses have not built civilizations. They have no language, and no technology that passes from one generation to the next. Why not?

Octopuses reproduce and then die. A female lays her eggs, guards them without eating, and gradually perishes before they hatch. Parent and offspring never meet. Everything she learned — the best hunting grounds, how to avoid predators, the trick for opening a tank lid — vanishes with her. The next generation starts from zero. As individuals, octopuses can be geniuses. As a collective, they have no memory. Knowledge that cannot accumulate cannot become civilization.

Primates took a different path. Extended periods of parental care meant that skills could be passed from parents to offspring. Language emerged within groups, and language became the technology for storing and transmitting experience. Writing arrived, and suddenly the storage capacity for knowledge exploded. An individual could die while the knowledge lived on. Intelligence became a property of the group rather than just the individual.

## Intelligence Is Ultimately a Question of Allocation

And here we arrive at a rather interesting conclusion. Intelligence is less about the absolute size of the brain than about what you do with the capacity you have.

Aquatic animals had to spend a substantial portion of their neural resources on three-dimensional movement control. Land animals shed that burden and could invest elsewhere. The octopus decentralized control of its boneless body and freed up its central brain. Primates invested heavily in social cognition, and language and culture emerged as a byproduct.

The brain is not free. The human brain accounts for about two percent of body weight but consumes twenty percent of the body's total energy. If twenty percent of your salary were going to a single expense, you would pay rather close attention to what that expense was. Nature works the same way — a brain only grows larger when there is a sufficiently good reason to pay for it.

Octopuses and humans followed entirely different evolutionary paths, but both arrived at the same core principle of intelligence: delegate the details, focus on what matters. The octopus delegated to its arms. Humans delegated to culture.

All of this from a fish that glided past me underwater. Writing this essay, I found myself developing something that might be called genuine respect for the octopus — a creature that distributes two-thirds of its brain to its limbs and still manages to conduct nocturnal heists and engineer aquarium escapes. I have my entire brain located in my head, the conventional and supposedly advantageous arrangement, and I couldn't tell you where I put my keys yesterday. The more you examine how nature has spent hundreds of millions of years solving the problem of intelligence, the harder it becomes not to feel a little humbled. Quite a lot humbled, actually.
