# The Horse on Gili Island and My Moral Circle

**A story about the expanding balloon of morality, which began with a horse-drawn cart on a tiny Indonesian island**

---

The first thing that strikes you when you arrive on the Gili Islands is the silence. No motorbikes. No car horns. The two-stroke engine drone that had burrowed into your eardrums all across Bali simply vanishes the moment you step off the boat, leaving only the sound of waves and the occasional rooster with poor time-management skills. Combustion engines are banned on the island. This single fact is enough to make a person's entire nervous system unclench.

But in this peaceful paradise, the only way to move luggage and people is by horse cart. They call it a *cidomo* -- a wooden carriage pulled by a single horse. And the moment I saw those horses, something snagged. Horses standing in the full blaze of the equatorial sun, waiting for passengers. Horses hauling heavy loads down sandy roads. Horses standing in the heat during their breaks too, because there was no shade. They didn't look horrifically mistreated. But the system itself -- the entire transportation infrastructure of this island -- was designed in a way that gave the horses no rest.

Here is an honest confession: it wasn't the discomfort that bothered me. It was the fact that I was uncomfortable at all. I grew up in rural Korea. Dogs guarded the house and ate table scraps, and everyone understood what eventually happened to them. Nobody attached any particular sentiment to the process. Animals were animals. People were people. That was the order of things.

And yet here I was, worrying about whether a horse was hot. When exactly did I become this person?

## A Thing Called the Moral Circle

The philosopher Peter Singer explains this phenomenon with what he calls the "Moral Circle" -- the idea that humans have a boundary around who they empathize with and whose rights they recognize, and that this boundary varies by society and era.

In the beginning, the circle was very small. Me and my family. My tribe, at best. Anyone outside the circle could be killed without guilt, enslaved without objection. Not so different, really, from the way I used to look at dogs back in the countryside. But the circle grew. Other tribes were recognized as human. Other races were granted rights. Women got to vote. Now the boundary is creeping past the species line entirely, reaching toward animals.

I have never read Singer's books. But what was happening inside me as I stared at those horses on Gili Island was precisely this. The circle had expanded. Not because I had willed it, but because at some point, society had trained me this way. Some would call it growth. Others would call it a luxury. I was simply bewildered.

## The Buddha Got There 2,500 Years Ago

Long before Singer brought up moral circles in modern ethics, the Buddha had already gone all the way. *All sentient beings.* Every creature capable of suffering is worthy of compassion. He set the radius of the circle to infinity and called it a day.

That was 2,500 years ago. Roughly 2,400 years before Peter Singer was born, a man sat under a Bodhi tree and settled the matter. This is fairly remarkable. But what is more remarkable is that this teaching has existed for two and a half millennia and the world is still, well, like this.

Knowing and living are different things. I know this. "All life is precious" is the sort of line you find in a children's textbook. And yet it is astonishingly easy to think this thought while grilling pork belly for lunch. The human brain is a magnificently convenient piece of engineering -- it can hold two contradictory beliefs in the same room and feel no tension whatsoever. I take some small comfort in the fact that this is not a personal failing but a feature of the entire species.

## Expanding the Circle Costs Money

Widening the moral circle is not a matter of sentiment. It is a matter of economics.

Raise chickens free-range instead of in battery cages, and the price of eggs doubles or triples. Replace horse carts on Gili with electric vehicles, and you need infrastructure investment and retraining costs for the drivers. Limit a horse's working hours to four a day, and the driver's income is cut in half. Every time the circle expands, someone gets the bill.

So every society draws a line somewhere. *This far and no further.* The placement of that line depends on wealth, culture, and era. Some countries have robust animal welfare laws. Others eat dogs. In America, dogs are family and cows are hamburgers. In India, cows are sacred and dogs live on the street. This is not a story about who is right and who is wrong. The lines are simply drawn in different places.

The tourist's circle is wide but weightless. The cart driver's circle is narrow but heavy. Which one is more real? Honestly, I have no idea.

## When Good Intentions Collide

Here is the interesting thing about Gili Island: everything that clashes here is well-intentioned.

They banned combustion engines to protect the environment. An excellent decision. But the result is that horses now shoulder all the transportation. A sweeping, planet-scale value -- environmental protection -- has climbed onto the back of an individual living creature. Calling this "eco-friendly" is a thought-provoking exercise in the use of language.

Tourists come to Gili because they love the quiet of a car-free island. They raise their cameras as a horse cart clatters down a sandy lane. They know the engine powering their romantic scene is a living animal standing in the merciless sun. What we wanted was an exotic landscape, and the horse was a component in it.

The drivers have their side too. A driver who cares about his horse and decides to work only four hours a day simply loses his passengers to the driver next door. An individual with good intentions, unable to act on them within the structure. This is not a battle between good and evil. It is a pile-up of good intentions at an intersection with no traffic lights.

## The Sound of Friction

In the end, what I felt on Gili Island was this: my line and this place's line were drawn in different spots.

At some point, I was trained into the kind of person who worries about animal suffering. The economy and culture of Gili Island draw the line at a place where human survival and livelihood come first. Both lines are rational within their own context. I cannot blame the driver. I cannot deny my own discomfort. This is not a question of larger and smaller. It is a question of different.

A boy who grew up in the Korean countryside feeling nothing about the fate of dogs now finds himself, decades later, worrying about horses on a small Indonesian island. The Buddha gave his answer 2,500 years ago, but I still eat pork belly. Ban the engines to save the planet, and the horses run in their place. Let the horses rest, and the driver's family goes hungry.

Travel may be, in the end, the act of stepping outside your own circle. And what you find out there is not just beautiful scenery, but the experience of seeing the shape of your own circle from the outside for the first time. There is no reason this should be pleasant. The outline that was invisible from inside becomes sharp and unmistakable when viewed from without.

The horses on Gili Island are probably still walking right now. I think about them being hot. And that thought makes me uncomfortable. The horses, presumably, are not uncomfortable about any of this. On this particular matter, they are better off than I am.
