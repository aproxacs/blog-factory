# What a Horse Taught Me About How Human Morality Actually Works

## A story about moral balloons, starting from a horse-drawn cart on a tiny Indonesian island

When I arrived on the Gili Islands, I was feeling pretty good about myself. A small Indonesian island with no motorized vehicles -- an eco-friendly paradise. Every travel blog called it "the last Eden on Earth," and I had finally set foot in it. I wouldn't go so far as to say I came with grand ambitions to reduce my carbon footprint, but I figured spending a few days on an island without car exhaust would make me feel like a slightly better human being. In the interest of full disclosure, I'm the kind of person who forgets to sort his recycling about one out of every three times. So the mere fact that I had traveled to an eco-island felt like a minor moral achievement.

That self-satisfaction lasted exactly fifteen minutes.

The mode of transport from the dock to my hotel was a horse-drawn cart. Which sounds romantic, in theory. The horses didn't look mistreated, either. Their coats were glossy, and they seemed healthy enough. The problem wasn't their condition -- it was the system. Under a merciless sun, these horses hauled luggage and tourists back and forth all day long, every day. Since combustion engines were banned, every ounce of freight fell on these animals. A well-intentioned environmental policy had, in practice, turned them into overworked beasts of burden. I grew up in the Korean countryside, on a farm with cows and chickens. The cows plowed the fields, the dogs guarded the house, and the chickens laid eggs until one day they became soup. I'm fairly accustomed to treating animals as tools. But even by my standards, this felt like too much.

As I sat on that cart, jolting along the dusty road, I found myself face to face with a deeply uncomfortable question: Why does this bother me?

## The Curious Balloon of Moral Concern

To understand where that discomfort came from, we need to meet Peter Singer, an Australian philosopher. In 1981, Singer introduced the concept of "the expanding circle" -- the idea that humanity's sphere of moral concern has been steadily growing throughout history.

In the beginning, humans cared only about their own family. When your child cried, your heart broke. When the neighbor's child cried, it was just noise. That was the size of morality for most of human history. Then the circle expanded to the tribe, then to the nation, then to the race, then across gender lines. Like someone blowing up a balloon, the circle kept growing. A few centuries ago, slavery was a perfectly legal institution, and denying women the vote was common sense. Say either of those things today and you'll be buried alive on the internet within minutes. In barely two hundred years, our moral balloon has inflated enormously.

And now, that balloon is reaching animals. We call our dogs family members, throw birthday parties for our cats, and hold funerals for our goldfish. Then we sit down to a steak dinner. We don't call this a contradiction. We call it culture. The discomfort I felt on the Gili Islands was the sensation of standing right at the expanding frontier of that balloon.

Here's the remarkable thing, though: someone inflated this balloon to cosmic proportions twenty-five hundred years ago. The Buddha. The Buddhist concept of the interconnection of all sentient beings declared that every living creature deserves moral consideration. Two thousand years before humanity was still fighting wars over whether other races counted as fully human, someone had already announced that even the ant is your sibling. What Peter Singer has been methodically expanding through rigorous argument, inch by careful inch, the Buddha apparently blew to infinity in a single meditation session. In fairness, it should be noted that the number of Buddhists who have actually lived by this teaching is vanishingly small compared to the number who have heard it. The distance between knowing the truth and living it is longer than the flight from the Gili Islands to Seoul. And I say this as someone who, whenever I try to meditate, starts debating dinner options within five minutes. I genuinely cannot fathom how that kind of enlightenment is even possible.

## Even Good Deeds Come with a Bill

But balloons have physical limits. Blow one up too far, and it pops. The same goes for the moral circle.

Economics has a concept called "diminishing marginal utility." The first slice of pizza is heaven. The second is great. The third is fine. By the seventh, you're questioning every life choice that led you to order a whole pizza. The expansion of the moral circle works similarly. Caring for your family is practically instinct -- it costs almost nothing. Caring for your neighbors is relatively easy, too. But worrying about the welfare of an unnamed shrimp on the other side of the planet? The cost rises exponentially while the additional moral satisfaction shrinks. Costs go up; emotional returns go down. This is the uncomfortable truth.

And this isn't just theory -- history proves it. The Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals (RSPCA) was founded in Britain in 1824. The National Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children (NSPCC) wasn't established until sixty years later. Animal protection was institutionalized before child protection. It sounds absurd, but there's a logic to it. Britain had amassed enormous wealth through the Industrial Revolution, and for the upper classes, the abuse of carriage horses was a visible, immediate problem. Child labor, on the other hand, was hidden behind factory walls, and children were regarded as the "property" of patriarchal households. The moral circle doesn't expand in logical order -- it expands in the order of visibility and affluence. We act on what we can see, and we reach out only when we can afford to.

## When Good Intentions Collide Head-On

Let's go back to the Gili Islands. The real problem with this island is that there are no villains.

Banning combustion engines was a decision made for the environment. The cart drivers work to feed their families. The tourists came to enjoy beautiful nature. Everyone has good intentions, but those good intentions collide, and the horses absorb the damage.

Environmentalists say, "Ban the engines." Animal rights activists say, "Stop overworking the horses." Both are right. But you can't satisfy both at the same time. Remove the engines and the horses suffer. Rest the horses and there's no way to move tourists around. This isn't a problem of individual morality. It's a problem of systems.

I'm hardly someone with expertise in moral philosophy -- I can barely manage everyday ethical decisions. I stand in the supermarket for thirty seconds agonizing between organic and conventional eggs, then grab whichever one is on sale. I feed my dog organic kibble while mindlessly eating factory-farmed chicken. If someone asked me what the difference is between a dog and a chicken, I'd struggle to answer. If it has a name, it's inside the moral circle. If it doesn't, it's outside. The horses on the Gili Islands surely had names. The cart drivers knew them. But to me, those horses were just transportation. And yet, having witnessed this structural dilemma firsthand, the realization that individual good intentions alone cannot change the world landed on me with real weight.

## Discomfort as an Engine

If the story ended here, it would be too depressing. Fortunately, history shows a more hopeful pattern.

There's a concept in philosophy called Hegel's dialectic: when a thesis and an antithesis collide, a synthesis emerges. I'll be honest -- when I first encountered this in a university lecture hall, I was mostly busy trying not to fall asleep. But the Gili Islands case shows the theory working with startling precision. When environmental protection (thesis) collided with animal welfare (antithesis), cracks began to appear. Petitions emerged calling for electric carts. Animal welfare organizations set up a permanent presence on the island to monitor the horses' health. There's still a long way to go, but without the collision, none of these movements would have started. When everything is comfortable, nothing changes.

History shows the same pattern again and again. In nineteenth-century London, the horse-drawn carriage industry was burying the city in manure. In 1894, The Times predicted that within fifty years, every street in London would be covered in nine feet of horse dung. What solved this problem wasn't a moral awakening -- it was technology. The automobile arrived. Of course, the automobile created its own problems, but at least the manure crisis was resolved. Problems always give birth to new solutions, and those solutions always create new problems. It might sound depressing, but the fact that this cycle keeps going is itself proof that we're moving forward.

Looking back, humanity's moral progress has always worked this way. Someone felt uncomfortable. There were people who felt uneasy owning slaves. People who felt uneasy barring women from the ballot box. People who felt uneasy sending children into factories. That discomfort didn't change the world overnight. But discomfort is contagious. One person's unease becomes ten people's unease, and ten people's unease becomes law, and law becomes culture.

## The Balloon Keeps Growing

Since returning from the Gili Islands, I sometimes think about those horses. They looked healthy, but they stood in the blazing sun without rest.

I've decided to take that discomfort as a good sign. Two hundred years ago, no one would have been bothered by that scene. Even fifty years ago, most people would have shrugged and said, "That's just what horses are for." The fact that we feel discomfort means the moral balloon has grown that much larger. I do worry sometimes that the balloon might burst, but so far, humanity's track record suggests it doesn't pop -- it just gets upgraded to a stronger material.

Of course, this realization doesn't immediately help the horses on the Gili Islands. But if thousands of tourists feel a similar pang of discomfort, and that discomfort gets written into reviews, and those reviews become news, and that news eventually leads to the introduction of electric carts -- a new synthesis -- then maybe it all started with a small, nagging feeling on the back of a horse-drawn cart.

Peter Singer's balloon keeps growing. It still has a long way to go before it reaches the Buddha's, but at least they're heading in the same direction. And I'm still the person who forgets to sort his recycling one time out of three, who grabs the eggs on sale instead of the organic ones, and who starts planning dinner five minutes into any meditation session. But thanks to one horse on the Gili Islands, I think I understand a little better now why the balloon needs to keep growing.

The horse never said a word. But I heard quite a lot.
