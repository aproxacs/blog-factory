# Stories, Golden Eggs

## The Person Who Couldn't Write

For a long time, I believed I couldn't write. More precisely, I thought I had decent ideas but couldn't express them. These feel like different problems, but they lead to the same place: nothing gets written.

There was clearly something in my head. I once spent a long time looking up why fish are bilaterally symmetrical. I'd noticed for years that advertisements were slowly disappearing from the Seoul subway. But whenever I tried to put any of this into words, something blocked me. The sentences came out stiff, or too ordinary, or simply boring when I read them back. So I gave up. The cycle went: leave it in the idea notebook, forget it exists. My notes app was an excellent cemetery.

Then I asked an AI to write for me. In the style of Bertrand Russell, one of my favorite writers. When I read the result, my brain lit up. I'm not exaggerating. It genuinely read like Russell — clear sentences, a wry twist of humor, the kind of writing that gets you nodding along and then quietly flips you over. It wasn't mine, obviously. But what I'd wanted to say was in there. It felt like stepping up to a karaoke machine and suddenly sounding like you can actually sing. Usually that's an illusion. This time, I played the recording back and it held up.

## What Dopamine Made Me Do

Without that rush, I probably would have stopped.

Because it was good, I did it again. Because I did it again, a structure emerged. Brainstorm, find the angle, let AI writer agents draft, have an AI editor pull it together. I called it [Blog Factory](https://github.com/aproxacs/blog-factory). The name suggests a factory. In practice it's closer to a one-person company where meetings keep running long.

Through this process, I discovered a writer named Bill Bryson. Or more accurately: I didn't know him, but an AI wrote a draft in his style. The move was something like — drop an impressive scientific fact, then immediately follow with "honestly, I had to sit with this for a while before I understood what it meant." Self-deprecation as humor. I encountered this for the first time through that draft. It was fresh. An AI had introduced me to a new author. I hadn't expected to get reading recommendations from an agent rather than an algorithm.

The writing wasn't mine. That's clear. But there was a real pleasure in watching the work take shape. Change the angle and the essay shifts. Change the subject and the tone follows. I found myself thinking: is this what writers feel?

That might be why this piece exists. The pleasure coexisted with a nagging question — is this actually my writing? And another: what exactly is the golden egg that makes a story compelling?

## So Is This My Writing?

This question stayed in my head for a while. Uncomfortably.

Some people reach for the film director analogy. Nolan doesn't operate the camera himself, but the film is still Nolan's. An architect doesn't carry bricks, but the design is still the architect's. So if an AI writes the sentences, but you provide the direction and the point of view, it's still yours.

That argument works. But I also wonder whether I like it partly because it's convenient. Directors have always worked within a division of labor — that's built into the form. Writers, traditionally, were people who sat alone and chose words. That definition itself is now changing.

The shift from "making it yourself" to "deciding the direction." Coding went through this first. Machine code to C, C to Python, and now "build me something that does this." At every step, someone probably asked whether this still counted as real programming. Today, saying that tends to make you sound a bit exhausting. Writing seems to be on the same path. Whether I'm standing at that turning point or just rationalizing toward comfort — I genuinely don't know. Possibly both. Humans tend to receive rationalization and insight in the same package.

## Everyone's Doing It and Nobody's Saying So

The definition of writing is already changing. Quietly, without disclosure.

A writer named Stephen Marche published a mystery novel built with three AI systems. He called himself an "orchestral conductor." At least he said it out loud. Japanese novelist Rie Kudan disclosed that about 5% of her Akutagawa Prize-winning novel consisted of sentences generated by ChatGPT. Five percent is a curiously precise number — small enough to say "barely," large enough to say "yes." Someone operating under the name Coral Heart publishes over 200 books a year under 21 pen names. At that volume, it's less literature and more like automating a fast-food chain.

Surveys suggest a significant portion of writers are already using AI. They just don't say so. When calculators first appeared, there must have been accountants who said they preferred to do it in their heads. We now know how briefly that lasted.

## Plastic Flowers

I'd already seen that AI can write well. The question is whether AI can — from formula alone, without human experience — produce writing that actually compels.

Stories have formulas. The hero's journey. Save the Cat. Setup, conflict, resolution, resonance. AI can follow these perfectly. It can produce something structurally impeccable.

But reading something written purely from formula feels off. Everything it says is correct, yet something is hollow. Like a plastic flower. The shape is there, the color is there, it looks like a flower. But there's no scent. Its advantage is that it never wilts — which is also precisely its flaw.

## The Actual Golden Eggs

I tried to work out why. Three things came to mind.

The first is the absence of a body. "It rained" and "I had no umbrella so I waited thirty minutes under the convenience store awning, and the rain was louder than I expected and I could barely hear my phone call" are different sentences. The second one has a body in it. A sensation that belongs to that specific person. AI has learned from an enormous amount of writing, but it has never been soaked anywhere.

The second is the combination of unlike things. Drawing evolutionary theory from fish symmetry. Connecting wild horses on a remote island to questions about moral responsibility. These connections require a particular life path and a few strange obsessions. AI extracts patterns, but genuinely unlikely combinations live outside the pattern.

The third is incompleteness. This sounds strange, but writing that's too smooth is oddly hard to read. A slight hesitation, a conclusion that lacks full conviction, a confession of "honestly, I'm not sure." AI tries to explain everything. It leaves no gaps. But writing that actually holds a reader's attention often lives in those gaps.

These three are what I currently think of as the golden eggs. Though I'm aware that the moment these get formalized, AI will follow along there too. I half-expect the next update to include a bead of sweat on the brow and a moment of genuine uncertainty. Whether these are permanent golden eggs or just the current gap — I can't say.

## Information Became Free

What's clear is that the value of information itself is behaving strangely.

Ask an AI anything and an answer comes back. But there's a difference between an answer that explains why fish are bilaterally symmetrical and a piece of writing that leaves some trace of feeling after you've read it. Knowing something and delivering it as experience are different problems, and the gap between them keeps widening.

The neuroscientist Jonathan Gottschall called stories "the brain's flight simulator" — a way to safely run another person's experience without having to live it yourself. If stories still have a job to do in an era of unlimited information, it might be that: converting data into experience.

## I Don't Really Know

Running Blog Factory, I noticed something odd. Sentences that include things I actually remember hold up far better than AI-generated drafts. It's not that the AI writes poorly. It's that the AI can't have had my experiences on my behalf. Even that slightly embarrassing rush I felt reading the first AI draft — that was mine too.

So: I have ideas. I now have tools to express them. What I seem to actually need is an inventory of experience. Noticing things, living through them, keeping track. If that's the real raw material of a story, it still has to be gathered with a body. The reason this conclusion bothers me most is that it's too wholesome.

I nearly wrote: in the age of AI, human experience has value. Then I stopped. It sounds too tidy. I'm a little suspicious of anything that tidy. Lucky for me there's an editor trained to distrust clean conclusions. The editor is also an AI, but still.
